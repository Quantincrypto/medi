# The EAC Gateway: Complete Guide to Importing Medical Equipment into Tanzania (2025)

Tanzania does not get the attention it deserves. Ask a medical equipment sales director to rank their African priorities and you will typically hear South Africa, Nigeria, Kenya — and then a pause. Tanzania sits in that pause, consistently underestimated by suppliers who have not looked closely at the numbers. A country of 63 million people, the fifth largest economy in Sub-Saharan Africa, the most stable political environment in East Africa, and a geographic position that makes it not just a market but a gateway to some of the most landlocked and supply-constrained healthcare systems on the continent. Importing medical equipment into Tanzania, once you understand how to navigate the TMDA registration process, is the kind of market entry decision that looks obvious in retrospect.

This guide is written for commercial and compliance professionals evaluating Tanzania for the first time. It covers the Tanzania Medicines and Medical Devices Authority (TMDA) registration process in full, customs clearance at Dar es Salaam Port and Julius Nyerere International Airport, the documentation requirements, fees in TZS and USD, and the regional distribution logic that makes Tanzania strategically valuable beyond its own domestic market.

## Why Tanzania Is the Underrated East African Medical Equipment Market

Tanzania's healthcare investment story has three distinct drivers that, together, make a compelling case for suppliers.

**Driver one: Population scale with low equipment penetration**

Tanzania's 63 million population is served by a healthcare infrastructure that, outside Dar es Salaam, Arusha, and a handful of regional cities, operates with equipment that is significantly below international standards. Diagnostic imaging outside major cities is limited. Laboratory automation is rare in district hospitals. Patient monitoring in regional surgical wards often relies on equipment that is well past its service life. This is not a criticism — it is a description of an unmet need that represents sustained procurement demand for the right suppliers.

**Driver two: Government investment through the Health Sector Strategic Plan**

Tanzania's Ministry of Health operates under a Health Sector Strategic Plan (HSSP) framework that has committed to upgrading district hospital diagnostic capacity, expanding regional referral hospital capabilities, and establishing a national laboratory network that meets international standards. World Bank and bilateral donor financing — including substantial investment from USAID, UKAID, and the Aga Khan Development Network — is flowing into this infrastructure upgrade. This is structured procurement, not ad-hoc purchasing.

**Driver three: Regional distribution platform**

This is where Tanzania's geography becomes strategically significant. Tanzania borders eight countries: Kenya, Uganda, Rwanda, Burundi, Democratic Republic of Congo, Zambia, Malawi, and Mozambique. Dar es Salaam Port is the primary import gateway for landlocked Rwanda, Burundi, eastern DRC, and Zambia. A supplier with a Tanzanian distribution base and TMDA registration is positioned to serve not just 63 million Tanzanians but a regional catchment of over 200 million people. No other single African market offers that combination of own-market demand and regional distribution reach.

## TMDA: Tanzania's Medical Device Regulator

The **Tanzania Medicines and Medical Devices Authority (TMDA)** is the statutory body responsible for regulating the quality, safety, and efficacy of medicines and medical devices in Tanzania. Established in 2019 under the Tanzania Medicines and Medical Devices Act (Cap. 219), TMDA replaced the Tanzania Food and Drugs Authority (TFDA) and brought a significantly strengthened focus on medical device regulation specifically.

TMDA sits under the Ministry of Health. Its medical device division oversees product registration, importer and distributor licensing, import permit issuance, and post-market surveillance. The authority has invested materially in regulatory capacity since its establishment — its online portal for product registration has improved substantially, its classification guidelines are publicly accessible, and its interaction with international manufacturers and importers is increasingly professional and predictable.

For suppliers who have operated in markets with older or more opaque regulatory frameworks, TMDA represents a genuinely positive surprise. It is not as streamlined as regulatory bodies in more mature markets, but its framework is coherent, its requirements are documented, and its staff have generally been receptive to technical dialogue with experienced international applicants and their local representatives.

**TMDA's regulatory philosophy:** TMDA aligns its device classification and technical dossier requirements with WHO and GHTF international standards. It accepts CE certification, US FDA clearance/approval, and TGA (Australia) registration as reference authorisations. This alignment with international frameworks reduces the additional documentation burden for manufacturers who are already certified in major markets.

## Medical Device Classification Under TMDA

TMDA classifies medical devices into four risk classes aligned with the WHO/GHTF framework:

**Class A — Minimal Risk**
Non-invasive devices with no contact with patient or only contact with intact skin. Examples: hospital furniture, non-sterile examination gloves, rulers, tongue depressors. Abbreviated notification pathway — fastest to process.

**Class B — Low Risk**
Devices with limited interaction with the body. Examples: hypodermic needles, non-sterile wound dressings, suction equipment, basic diagnostic instruments. Standard registration documentation required.

**Class C — Moderate Risk**
Devices with more significant patient interaction. Examples: infusion pumps, patient monitors, diagnostic ultrasound, X-ray systems, lung ventilators, haematology analysers. Full technical dossier required — this is where most capital medical equipment sits.

**Class D — High Risk**
Highest risk devices, typically implantable or life-sustaining. Examples: implantable cardiac devices, HIV diagnostic test kits, blood glucose monitoring systems, sterile single-use invasive devices. Most extensive documentation and longest review timelines.

## Step-by-Step TMDA Registration Process

### Step 1: Appoint a Licensed Tanzanian Importer/Agent

Foreign manufacturers cannot apply to TMDA directly. You must appoint a TMDA-licensed importer who holds a valid Medical Device Importer Licence and who will act as your authorised representative in Tanzania. This entity submits the registration application, holds the import permits, and takes regulatory responsibility for the product in the Tanzanian market. The quality of your importer partner directly determines your registration timeline and ongoing compliance management.

### Step 2: Confirm Device Classification

Submit a pre-classification query to TMDA or work with your importer to confirm the correct device class before preparing your dossier. Misclassification — particularly underclassifying a Class C device as Class B — is a common cause of rejection and restarts the process.

### Step 3: Prepare the Technical Dossier

The TMDA registration dossier includes:

- TMDA application form (completed and signed by the licensed importer)
- Manufacturer authorisation letter appointing the Tanzanian importer
- Certificate of Free Sale from country of manufacture (apostilled)
- CE certificate, US FDA clearance, or TGA registration (as applicable)
- ISO 13485 quality management certificate (current)
- Instructions for Use — English language (Swahili translation may be required for certain Class C and all Class D devices)
- Technical specifications and labelling samples
- Summary of safety and clinical/performance data
- Post-market surveillance data (Class C and D)
- List of countries where the device is currently marketed

### Step 4: Submit via TMDA Portal

TMDA has developed an online submission portal for product registration. Applications are submitted by the licensed importer through this portal, with supporting documents uploaded in PDF format. TMDA assigns an application tracking number and sends acknowledgement of receipt.

### Step 5: Administrative Screening

TMDA conducts an initial administrative review to confirm the dossier is complete. Incomplete applications are rejected at this stage with a query list — the most efficient path is a complete, well-organised dossier on first submission. The screening stage typically takes 2–4 weeks.

### Step 6: Technical Evaluation

The technical evaluation is conducted by TMDA's medical device assessment team. For Class A and B devices, evaluation is typically documentation-based. For Class C devices, the evaluator may request additional technical clarification or performance data. For Class D devices, a full expert panel review may be convened.

### Step 7: Registration Certificate Issuance

Upon approval, TMDA issues a Medical Device Registration Certificate. The certificate is product-specific and importer-specific, valid for five years, and required for all subsequent import permits. Renewal must be initiated before expiry.

## Customs Clearance: Dar es Salaam Port and Julius Nyerere Airport

### Dar es Salaam Port

Dar es Salaam Port is the dominant entry point for medical equipment imports into Tanzania. It is also the principal maritime gateway for landlocked Rwanda, Burundi, eastern DRC, and Zambia — which means the port handles significant transit cargo in addition to Tanzania-destined shipments. The Tanzania Ports Authority (TPA) manages port operations, and Tanzania Revenue Authority (TRA) customs officials process import clearances.

The standard clearance process for medical equipment imports:

1. **Arrival notification** — shipping agent notifies the Tanzanian importer of vessel arrival and cargo availability
2. **Pre-arrival documentation** — submission of Bill of Lading, commercial invoice, packing list, TMDA import permit, and registration certificate copy to TRA customs via the Tanzania Integrated Customs Management System (TANCIS)
3. **Customs classification** — medical equipment typically enters under HS Chapter 90. Accurate HS code determination before shipment is essential — misclassification triggers queries and delays
4. **Scanning and inspection** — TRA may require physical inspection or scanning of the container. For medical equipment consignments with proper documentation, inspection is typically non-intrusive
5. **Duty calculation and payment** — Tanzania applies the East African Community Common External Tariff (EAC CET). Medical equipment classified under the EAC CET receives a 0% base import duty in many categories. Confirm applicable rates with your freight forwarder
6. **Port clearance and delivery** — well-documented shipments typically clear within 5–10 working days of vessel arrival. Delays at Dar es Salaam Port, while less common than at some regional peers, can extend clearance for congested vessels

### Julius Nyerere International Airport (JNIA)

Julius Nyerere International Airport in Dar es Salaam handles air freight for time-sensitive or high-value consignments. Cargo clearance at JNIA follows the same TANCIS-based customs process as the seaport. Air freight is significantly faster than sea freight for urgent equipment needs — typically 2–5 days clearance for compliant shipments — but substantially more expensive per kilogram.

### The Northern Corridor: Arusha and Kilimanjaro

For suppliers serving northern Tanzania, Uganda, Rwanda, and Burundi, the Northern Corridor via Mombasa Port (Kenya) and the A109 highway to Arusha is an alternative surface freight route. Kilimanjaro International Airport near Arusha also handles cargo for the northern region. Coordinate with your freight forwarder on corridor selection based on destination and timeline.

## Practical Challenges Unique to Tanzania

**Port Congestion at Dar es Salaam**

Dar es Salaam Port is one of the busiest in East Africa. During peak periods, vessel waiting times and container dwell times increase. Plan consignments with buffer time and use pre-arrival clearance mechanisms available through TANCIS to reduce port-side delays.

**Swahili Language Requirements**

While English is widely used in business and government, Swahili is the national language and is increasingly required in consumer-facing labelling and patient-directed Instructions for Use for certain device categories. Work with your importer to confirm labelling requirements before producing final packaging.

**Infrastructure Outside Dar es Salaam**

Tanzania's road network has improved significantly but remains variable in rural areas. Equipment destined for regional hospitals — particularly in the Lake Zone (Mwanza), Southern Highlands (Mbeya), or the Tanzanian interior — requires careful logistics planning. Include installation and service logistics in your market entry plan.

**Tanzania Zanzibar Regulatory Consideration**

Zanzibar — the semi-autonomous island region — has its own administrative framework. Medical equipment imports destined for Zanzibar hospitals may require additional coordination with Zanzibar-specific health authorities. Confirm with your importer whether your target buyers are on the mainland or in Zanzibar, as the import pathway differs slightly.

## Tanzania as a Regional Distribution Hub

This is the dimension of the Tanzanian market that most first-time entrants underestimate. Dar es Salaam Port's geographic position means it serves as the de facto import gateway for:

- **Rwanda** — 100% landlocked; Dar es Salaam is the nearest deep-water port via the TANZAM corridor
- **Burundi** — primary port access via Dar es Salaam and Kigoma (Lake Tanganyika ferry)
- **Eastern DRC** — Dar es Salaam serves eastern DRC via lake and road connections through Kigoma and Uvira
- **Zambia** — the TANZAM corridor (Tanzania-Zambia road and the TAZARA railway) connects Dar es Salaam directly to Zambia

A supplier who establishes a TMDA-registered Tanzanian distribution partner is not only serving Tanzania's 63 million people — they are creating a logistics infrastructure that can serve the healthcare needs of the broader Great Lakes and Southern African corridor. The investment in TMDA registration pays dividends across multiple markets.

The EAC (East African Community) framework, of which Tanzania is a founding member, is progressively harmonising regulatory requirements across member states. Suppliers who engage with Tanzania early are positioning themselves advantageously for the EAC harmonised medical device regulatory framework that is expected to mature over the coming years.

## Practical Tips for First-Time Market Entrants

**1. Choose an importer with TMDA relationship depth, not just a licence**
Any importer can hold a TMDA licence. What matters is the depth of their relationship with TMDA's medical device assessment team and their experience with your specific device category. Ask for examples of Class C or Class D registrations they have completed, and ask for manufacturer references.

**2. Use pre-arrival clearance at Dar es Salaam Port**
Tanzania's TANCIS system allows importers to submit customs clearance documentation before the vessel arrives. Importers who use pre-arrival clearance consistently achieve significantly faster port release times. Confirm with your importer that they routinely use this mechanism.

**3. Price in USD, hold USD**
Tanzania Shilling (TZS) is relatively stable compared to some African currencies, but medical equipment transactions — particularly for higher-value capital equipment — are typically denominated in USD. Structure your distributor agreements and invoicing in USD where possible.

**4. Target mission hospitals and private diagnostic centres first**
Mission hospitals (run by the Catholic, Anglican, and other church-affiliated networks) and private diagnostic centres in Dar es Salaam, Arusha, and Mwanza typically have more accessible procurement processes than government hospital systems. They make procurement decisions faster, often have USD access, and provide reference installations that strengthen your position in subsequent government tenders.

**5. Plan for installation and service, not just supply**
Tanzania's hospital procurement authorities are increasingly asking suppliers for post-sale service commitments. An equipment supplier who can demonstrate authorised service partner coverage in Tanzania, or who includes a service contract in the supply scope, has a measurable advantage in tender evaluations.

**6. Register with the EAC Medicines and Medical Devices Portal**
The East African Community is progressively building a regional medical device database. Engaging with EAC harmonisation mechanisms early — even before they are fully operational — signals commitment to the regional market and positions your products favourably as harmonisation matures.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long does TMDA medical device registration take?

Typical timelines: Class A, 2–3 months. Class B, 3–5 months. Class C, 5–8 months. Class D, 8–12 months. These assume a complete, professionally prepared dossier submitted by an experienced licensed importer. Incomplete submissions trigger a query process that adds months to the timeline. Budget conservatively, particularly for capital equipment in Class C.

### Do I need a local Tanzanian agent to register with TMDA?

Yes. Foreign manufacturers cannot submit TMDA registration applications directly. A TMDA-licensed importer must act as the authorised representative, submit the application, hold the import permits, and maintain regulatory accountability for the product in Tanzania. The choice of importer is the most consequential decision in your Tanzanian market entry process.

### What import duties apply to medical equipment in Tanzania?

Tanzania applies the EAC Common External Tariff. Many medical equipment categories classified under HS Chapter 90 attract 0% base import duty under the EAC CET framework. However, VAT (currently 18%), port levies, and other charges apply. Confirm applicable rates for your specific HS codes with an ERCA-experienced freight forwarder before your first shipment.

### How does Tanzania's registration compare to Kenya's PPB process?

Both TMDA and Kenya's PPB require local importer representation and a similar technical dossier. TMDA registration timelines are broadly comparable to PPB. TMDA's online portal is functional and increasingly efficient. The key practical difference is that Kenya's PPB framework has a longer track record and more established enforcement precedent. For suppliers evaluating East Africa, registering in both Tanzania and Kenya is advisable — together they cover the two largest EAC markets and provide a regional distribution foundation.

### Can I use Tanzania as a distribution hub for Rwanda, Burundi, and Zambia?

Yes — this is one of Tanzania's most compelling strategic advantages. Dar es Salaam Port is the primary maritime gateway for Rwanda, Burundi, eastern DRC, and Zambia. Establishing a TMDA-registered distribution partner creates a logistics infrastructure that meaningfully supports the entire Great Lakes and Southern African corridor. Note that TMDA registration does not grant automatic market access in these neighbouring countries — each requires separate regulatory compliance.

### What medical equipment is in highest demand in Tanzania?

Current procurement demand is concentrated in: diagnostic imaging (ultrasound, X-ray — district hospital expansion is the primary driver); laboratory equipment (haematology analysers, biochemistry platforms — national laboratory network targets); patient monitoring (surgical wards, ICU expansion); dialysis equipment (renal disease burden growing with urbanisation); maternal and neonatal health equipment (delivery suites, neonatal incubators — sustained government priority); and dental equipment (private clinic sector growing rapidly in Dar es Salaam and Arusha).

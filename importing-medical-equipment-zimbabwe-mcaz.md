# The Rebuilding Market: Complete Guide to Importing Medical Equipment into Zimbabwe (2025)

Zimbabwe presents a market opportunity that reads differently from every other guide in this series. This is not a frontier market like Ethiopia where the regulatory framework is still maturing, nor a stable high-volume market like South Africa where the competition is intense. Zimbabwe is something more specific: a healthcare system in active reconstruction, with an acute equipment deficit accumulated over two decades of economic stress, a USD-denominated economy that resolves many of the payment challenges that afflict other African markets, and a government that has identified healthcare infrastructure as a national rebuilding priority.

For medical equipment suppliers willing to engage with a market that has real complexity, the strategic picture is compelling. Importing medical equipment into Zimbabwe requires navigating the Medicines Control Authority of Zimbabwe (MCAZ), but the registration process — while not the fastest on the continent — is navigable, the market's equipment needs are significant and largely unmet, and the shift to USD commerce has addressed the foreign exchange problem that historically made trading with Zimbabwe extremely difficult.

This guide is written for suppliers who are ready to take Zimbabwe seriously. It covers MCAZ registration in full, customs clearance at Robert Gabriel Mugabe International Airport and Beitbridge Border Post, fees in USD, the practical challenges specific to this market, and the rebuilding investment context that is driving procurement demand.

## Why Zimbabwe Is a Rebuilding Market Worth Considering Now

Zimbabwe's healthcare system was, in the 1980s and early 1990s, among the best in Sub-Saharan Africa. The University of Zimbabwe College of Health Sciences trained physicians who were sought across the region. National hospitals operated with equipment that matched regional referral standards. The collapse of that system from the late 1990s through the 2010s — driven by hyperinflation, capital flight, and the emigration of healthcare professionals — is well documented.

What is less well documented is the recovery trajectory. Zimbabwe's formal dollarisation — the country abandoned its own currency entirely in 2015 and operates a USD-dominated economy, with the Zimbabwe Gold (ZiG) currency introduced in 2024 — resolved the most acute payment mechanism problem for international suppliers. Equipment procurement can now be contracted and paid in USD with a reliability that did not exist during the hyperinflation era.

**The demand driver: an acute equipment deficit**

Two decades of constrained capital investment have left Zimbabwe's hospital network operating with equipment that is significantly life-expired. Parirenyatwa Group of Hospitals and Harare Central Hospital — the country's principal referral facilities — have been the subject of major government-backed refurbishment programmes. The National Referral Hospital Revitalisation Programme, supported by the government and international partners, is procuring diagnostic imaging, ICU equipment, surgical systems, and laboratory infrastructure at scale. District and provincial hospitals face similar deficits with similar procurement pipelines.

**Private sector resilience**

Zimbabwe's private healthcare sector — led by hospitals such as Avenues Clinic, West End Hospital, and Mater Dei Hospital in Harare, and several facilities in Bulawayo — maintained a higher standard of care throughout the economic crisis by prioritising USD-denominated fee collection and reinvestment in equipment. This private segment has continued to grow, driven by diaspora remittances and employer-funded health insurance schemes. Private hospitals in Harare and Bulawayo purchase equipment at a pace and with a decision-making speed that compares favourably to any market in the region.

**The diaspora factor**

Zimbabwe has one of the largest diaspora populations relative to its national population of any African country — approximately 3–4 million Zimbabweans live abroad, primarily in South Africa, the UK, the USA, Canada, and Australia. Diaspora remittances are a significant driver of healthcare spending, and diaspora-invested private clinics and diagnostic centres are a growing procurement segment. This demographic profile creates a buyer segment that is internationally connected, has access to hard currency, and has direct knowledge of international equipment standards.

## MCAZ: Zimbabwe's Medical Device Regulator

The **Medicines Control Authority of Zimbabwe (MCAZ)** is the statutory body responsible for regulating medicines and medical devices in Zimbabwe. MCAZ was established under the Medicines and Allied Substances Control Act (Chapter 15:03) and operates under the Ministry of Health and Child Care. Its mandate covers product registration, import permit authorisation, quality surveillance, and post-market monitoring.

MCAZ is an established institution — it has been operational for over two decades and has developed a body of practice around medical device registration that, while more modest in scale than SAHPRA or NAFDAC, is coherent and documented. The authority has modernised its submission processes progressively and engages professionally with international manufacturers and their local representatives.

**MCAZ's regulatory alignment:** MCAZ accepts CE certification, US FDA clearance/approval, and WHO Prequalification as primary reference authorisations. Its classification framework follows WHO/GHTF guidance. Manufacturers who are already certified in major international markets will find that their existing documentation portfolio largely meets MCAZ's technical requirements.

**Registration scope:** MCAZ registers medical devices separately from medicines. The medical device registration pathway has been clarified and updated progressively since 2015. Products registered with MCAZ are listed on the MCAZ register — this is the document your Zimbabwean importer needs to present for import permit applications.

## Medical Device Classification Under MCAZ

MCAZ uses a four-class risk classification system aligned with the GHTF framework:

**Class I — Low Risk**
Non-invasive devices with minimal patient contact. Examples: hospital furniture, non-sterile examination gloves, stethoscopes without electronic components, basic bandages. Abbreviated registration pathway.

**Class II — Low to Medium Risk**
Devices with limited or indirect patient contact. Examples: non-implantable accessories, basic diagnostic instruments, non-sterile wound dressings. Standard registration pathway.

**Class III — Medium to High Risk**
Devices with significant patient interaction or used in diagnosis and treatment. Examples: diagnostic ultrasound, X-ray systems, patient monitors, infusion pumps, anaesthesia machines, laboratory analysers. Full technical dossier required. This classification covers most capital medical equipment.

**Class IV — High Risk**
Highest risk, typically life-sustaining or implantable. Examples: implantable cardiac devices, HIV diagnostic test kits, in vitro diagnostic devices for blood transfusion screening. Most extensive documentation, specialist review, longest timelines.

## Step-by-Step MCAZ Registration Process

### Step 1: Appoint a Licensed Zimbabwean Agent/Importer

MCAZ requires that foreign manufacturers appoint a Zimbabwe-registered entity as their local authorised agent. This agent submits the registration application, maintains regulatory correspondence with MCAZ, holds the import authorisations, and is accountable for the product's compliance in the Zimbabwean market. A Zimbabwean agent with established MCAZ relationships and experience in your device category is the most important enabler of a smooth registration.

### Step 2: Product Classification Confirmation

Confirm the correct MCAZ classification with your agent before preparing the dossier. Where classification is ambiguous, MCAZ will accept pre-submission classification queries. Getting classification right on first submission is essential — reclassification after rejection adds significant time.

### Step 3: Compile the Technical Registration Dossier

Required documentation:

- MCAZ application form — completed by the Zimbabwean authorised agent
- Manufacturer's letter of authorisation appointing the Zimbabwean agent (notarised)
- Certificate of Free Sale from the country of manufacture
- CE certificate, US FDA clearance, or WHO Prequalification (as applicable)
- ISO 13485 quality management certificate (valid and current)
- Instructions for Use — English language (Zimbabwe's official language of commerce)
- Technical specifications including power requirements (Zimbabwe uses 220V/50Hz)
- Labelling samples
- Summary of clinical/performance data
- Post-market surveillance data (Class III and IV)
- List of countries where the device is currently registered and marketed

### Step 4: Submit Application to MCAZ

Applications are submitted to MCAZ's medical devices division in Harare. MCAZ has been developing its digital submission capabilities — confirm with your agent whether current submissions are online, in person, or hybrid. MCAZ assigns an application reference number upon receipt.

### Step 5: Administrative and Technical Review

MCAZ conducts an initial administrative review for completeness, followed by a technical evaluation. Class I and II devices with complete dossiers typically pass through screening efficiently. Class III devices receive a more detailed technical assessment, which may include requests for additional clarification or performance data. Class IV devices receive the most rigorous review.

### Step 6: Registration Certificate Issuance

Upon approval, MCAZ issues a Medical Device Registration Certificate. This certificate is product-specific and agent-specific, is listed on the MCAZ register, and is required for import permit applications. Registration is valid for five years and must be renewed before expiry.

## MCAZ Fee Schedule (2025 Estimates)

Zimbabwe's USD economy means MCAZ fees are denominated in USD — a notable difference from most other African regulators whose fees are in local currency and subject to exchange rate volatility. Fees are subject to revision by MCAZ.

## Customs Clearance: Harare and Beitbridge

### Robert Gabriel Mugabe International Airport, Harare

Robert Gabriel Mugabe International Airport in Harare is the primary entry point for air freight medical equipment imports. The Zimbabwe Revenue Authority (ZIMRA) manages customs clearance at the airport. The standard clearance process:

1. **Arrival notification** — freight agent notifies importer of cargo availability
2. **Documentation lodgement** — commercial invoice, airway bill, packing list, MCAZ import permit, and product registration certificate presented to ZIMRA
3. **Customs classification** — medical equipment enters under HS Chapter 90. Accurate HS code determination before shipment reduces the risk of classification disputes at clearance
4. **Inspection and release** — ZIMRA may conduct physical inspection. For medical equipment with full documentation, inspection is typically administrative
5. **Duty and VAT assessment** — Zimbabwe applies duty and VAT to imports. Medical equipment may qualify for duty exemptions under specific procurement categories — confirm applicable rates for your HS codes before shipment
6. **Clearance and delivery** — compliant, well-documented shipments typically clear within 3–7 working days at the airport

### Beitbridge Border Post (South Africa–Zimbabwe)

Beitbridge is the busiest land border crossing in southern Africa, connecting South Africa and Zimbabwe on the N1/A6 highway corridor. For medical equipment suppliers shipping from South Africa or routing cargo via South African ports (Durban, Port Elizabeth), Beitbridge is the primary surface freight entry point. The border has historically been associated with significant congestion — transit times can extend significantly during peak periods. The One-Stop Border Post (OSBP) infrastructure improvements implemented in recent years have reduced delays, but suppliers should factor variable clearance times into their logistics planning.

### Joshua Mqabuko Nkomo Airport, Bulawayo

Bulawayo — Zimbabwe's second city and the commercial hub of Matabeleland — is served by Joshua Mqabuko Nkomo International Airport. For equipment destined for Bulawayo hospitals and the southern region, Bulawayo airport cargo handling is an alternative to routing everything through Harare.

## Practical Challenges Specific to Zimbabwe

**The ZiG Currency Transition**

Zimbabwe's monetary situation has been complex. The country adopted USD as its primary commercial currency in 2015 after abandoning successive domestic currencies. In 2024, Zimbabwe introduced the Zimbabwe Gold (ZiG) as a new structured currency. As of 2025, USD remains the dominant currency for medical equipment procurement, invoicing, and payment. Verify current currency acceptance with your Zimbabwean agent before committing to payment terms, and ensure your contracts specify USD.

**Healthcare Professional Retention**

Zimbabwe's medical and technical workforce has experienced significant emigration. Many hospitals face challenges not only with equipment supply but with the technical expertise to operate sophisticated diagnostic and therapeutic equipment. Suppliers whose offering includes installation, operator training, and ongoing technical support have a distinct advantage in Zimbabwean procurement. Build a service and training component into your supply proposal — it is increasingly a differentiating factor in tender evaluations.

**Infrastructure and Power Supply**

Zimbabwe's electricity supply has been subject to significant load-shedding, particularly in regional cities and districts outside Harare and Bulawayo. Medical equipment for Zimbabwean hospitals — particularly equipment in critical care, laboratory, and imaging categories — should be specified with UPS systems, surge protection, and power conditioning appropriate for an unstable grid environment. This is not optional; it is a clinical safety requirement. Include it in your standard supply scope for this market.

**Government Procurement Process**

Public hospital procurement in Zimbabwe runs through the State Procurement Board (SPB) and individual hospital procurement committees. Government procurement can be slower than private sector purchasing and is subject to budget release timing. Align your pipeline with the Zimbabwean government budget cycle (January–December) and with donor-funded procurement timelines where applicable.

## The National Referral Hospital Revitalisation Programme

Zimbabwe's Ministry of Health and Child Care has identified the national referral hospital network as a primary investment target. The **National Referral Hospital Revitalisation Programme** encompasses Parirenyatwa Group of Hospitals and Harare Central Hospital in Harare, Mpilo Central Hospital in Bulawayo, and several provincial hospitals. The programme has attracted financing from the Government of Zimbabwe's own capital budget and from bilateral partners including China (equipment and infrastructure packages), the African Development Bank, and humanitarian partners.

The equipment procurement associated with this programme spans: diagnostic imaging (CT, MRI, ultrasound, X-ray); laboratory modernisation (haematology, biochemistry, microbiology, blood bank); ICU and high-dependency unit equipment; operating theatre fit-outs; and patient monitoring infrastructure.

For suppliers, the revitalisation programme represents structured, large-scale procurement with defined timelines. Engagement typically requires MCAZ product registration, local agent representation, and alignment with tender processes managed by the Ministry of Health or its appointed procurement agent. Suppliers with established relationships with Zimbabwe's Ministry of Health procurement officials — or with the international partners financing specific sub-programmes — have a material advantage.

## Practical Tips for First-Time Market Entrants

**1. Lead with refurbished, ISO 13485-certified equipment**
Zimbabwe's procurement budget constraints mean that competitively priced, high-quality refurbished equipment from certified suppliers is often preferred over new equipment. The key is certification — ISO 13485, CE marking, and a credible warranty and service package. Do not offer the cheapest uncertified equipment; the market has learned from experience that this is a false economy.

**2. Private hospitals first, government second**
Avenues Clinic, West End Hospital, and the growing private diagnostic sector in Harare make procurement decisions quickly and pay in USD. Establish reference sites in private facilities before pursuing government tender opportunities — references from credible Zimbabwean private hospitals carry weight in government procurement evaluations.

**3. Include training and service in your proposal**
Zimbabwe's healthcare technical workforce deficit is real. Suppliers who include operator training, biomedical technician training, and a first-year service contract as part of their standard offering win more tenders. This is not a concession — it is a differentiator.

**4. Work with a Zimbabwean agent who understands both MCAZ and ZIMRA**
Regulatory compliance and customs clearance are distinct skill sets. Some agents are excellent at MCAZ registration but less experienced at navigating ZIMRA clearance efficiently. Evaluate both capabilities when selecting your local partner.

**5. Factor load-shedding into your equipment specification**
Do not assume stable power supply outside Harare's CBD. UPS systems, solar-compatible power interfaces, and voltage stabilisation are procurement requirements for district and provincial hospitals, not optional add-ons.

**6. Connect with the Zimbabwean diaspora channel**
Diaspora-connected procurement — where diaspora Zimbabweans in the UK, USA, South Africa, and Australia facilitate equipment purchases for family clinics or community hospitals — is a real and growing channel. Suppliers who establish relationships with Zimbabwean professional associations and diaspora health networks in these countries tap into a procurement channel that moves faster and with fewer bureaucratic constraints than formal government procurement.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long does MCAZ medical device registration take?

Typical timelines are: Class I, 2–4 months; Class II, 3–5 months; Class III, 5–9 months; Class IV, 9–14 months. These timelines assume a complete dossier submitted by an experienced Zimbabwean authorised agent. Incomplete submissions trigger queries that add months. For capital equipment in Class III, budget 6 months minimum.

### Do I need a local Zimbabwean agent to register with MCAZ?

Yes. Foreign manufacturers must appoint a Zimbabwe-registered authorised agent who submits the registration application, maintains MCAZ correspondence, and holds import authorisations. The agent's quality — specifically their MCAZ experience and their relationship with the authority's medical devices division — significantly influences your registration timeline.

### What currency is used for medical equipment transactions in Zimbabwe?

USD is the dominant currency for medical equipment procurement and payment in Zimbabwe. The Zimbabwe Gold (ZiG) was introduced in 2024 but USD remains standard for capital equipment transactions. Invoice and contract in USD. Confirm current currency arrangements with your agent before finalising payment terms.

### What are the main challenges importing medical equipment into Zimbabwe?

Four challenges consistently affect first-time market entrants: (1) Power supply instability — specify UPS and power conditioning for all equipment destined for regional hospitals. (2) Government procurement timing — public hospital procurement aligns with budget release cycles and can be unpredictable. (3) Technical workforce gaps — include operator training in your supply scope. (4) ZiG currency transition uncertainty — confirm USD payment mechanisms with your agent before contractual commitments.

### How does Zimbabwe compare to South Africa as a medical equipment market?

They are very different markets. South Africa has a larger, more mature market with significantly more competition, a fully sophisticated regulatory framework in SAHPRA, and strong private hospital groups who are experienced buyers. Zimbabwe has acute equipment needs, less competition, a more accessible private sector procurement process, and a USD economy that simplifies payments. For suppliers already established in South Africa, Zimbabwe is a logical neighbouring market expansion — the Beitbridge corridor and geographic proximity make it practical to serve both markets from a South African logistics base.

### Is Zimbabwe a good market for refurbished medical equipment?

Yes — Zimbabwe is one of the clearest market fits for high-quality, ISO 13485-certified refurbished equipment on the African continent. Budget constraints across both public and private sectors mean that certified refurbished equipment offers the optimal combination of clinical performance and procurement cost. The key is certification and warranty support — buyers have learned to distinguish between certified refurbished equipment from reputable suppliers and uncertified second-hand equipment, and they pay a premium for the former.
